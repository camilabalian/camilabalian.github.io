Myriad

Apple's internal teams were stuck exporting dashboards to slides just to share a number. I designed three of the five core flows for the reporting tool that let them stop passing around screenshots and start owning their data.

Client
Apple GBI
Role
Product Designer
Team
7 designers · 1 PM
Year
2025
Overview

The brief

Apple GBI needed a reporting tool to replace rigid dashboards that couldn't be customised or shared without exporting to slides. The tool had to feel native to Apple without copying existing products, serve both Admins who build dashboards and Users who consume data, and pass internal accessibility review at every level.

The team was eight people: seven designers and a PM. I owned the UI for three of five core flows (onboarding, dashboard editing, and sharing), co-led the brand exploration, and built core parts of the design system.

Research

What I heard.

The team interviewed teams across Apple GBI. Three themes kept surfacing: the need for flexibility, the demand for context behind numbers, and the desire for each team to own their data view.

Visual system

Brand, colour, components.

I explored three brand directions and co-developed the winner: a toggle-switch "M" in blue, cyan, and pink that carried through the entire system. I defined the colour palette (derived from iOS system colours), built the WCAG 2.0 accessibility matrix, and designed core components of the 40+ piece library built to survive handoff.

What shipped

The three flows I designed.

01 First Use

Users didn't know what the tool could do.

No onboarding existed. People figured it out or gave up. I designed the first-run experience that asks "How will you use Myriad?" and adapts the interface based on the answer. I tested walkthroughs and feature tours first, but both overwhelmed users with things they didn't care about yet. Self-selection won because it let people skip straight to what was relevant to them.

02 Dashboard

Building a dashboard felt like writing SQL.

Stakeholders wanted a technical configuration panel. Users wanted to drag things and see what happens. The team chose the Keynote model: a visual grid with real-time editing. I designed the sidebar panel that lets you configure a widget without leaving the canvas, because every time users lost sight of their dashboard during editing, they lost context. Four widget sizes was a deliberate constraint: enough flexibility to be useful, not enough to create visual chaos across teams.

03 Share

Sharing meant exporting a PDF and hoping for the best.

The old workflow: export, email, wait for sign-off, repeat quarterly. I designed the in-app sharing flow that replaced this, including configurable approval schedules per person, because not every stakeholder operates at the same cadence. A VP reviewing monthly doesn't need the same notifications as a team lead checking weekly. This addressed the single most common complaint from research.

Impact

What it delivered.

Adoption
12 teams onboarded
Teams across Apple GBI moved from static exports and email chains to building, customising, and sharing dashboards themselves within the first quarter of launch.
Efficiency
Reporting time cut by 80%
What used to take days (exporting, formatting slides, emailing for sign-off) now happened in minutes with in-app sharing and scheduled approvals.
Design system
40+ components shipped
A documented component library with four widget sizes, full colour accessibility, and interaction guidelines, built by the team so the product could scale after handoff.
Takeaways

What this project taught me.

Design familiar before you design new.

The biggest unlock wasn't a novel interaction. It was making dashboard editing feel like Keynote. The hardest design work is often invisible.

Design for the person who inherits your file.

I wouldn't maintain Myriad forever. The component library shipped with documented states and usage rules because the next designer shouldn't have to reverse-engineer my decisions.
