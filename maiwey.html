Overview

The challenge

Maiwey's parent company had decades of B2B financial experience but had never spoken directly to retail customers. They needed everything from scratch: brand, public marketing website, private investment platform, and mobile app. In a market where incumbents already owned the "simple fintech" positioning.

The team was three designers: a UX researcher, a designer who doubled as PM, and me as product designer across the full scope: research, brand, product UI, and documentation.

Discovery

What the research told us.

I ran workshops with stakeholders and interviews with potential users. Three findings shaped every decision after:

People didn't distrust fintech. They distrusted complexity. The barrier was jargon, hidden fees, and interfaces that assumed financial literacy.

Transparency was the missing feature. Users said they'd move their money if they could actually see what was happening to it: real numbers, clear timelines, no surprises.

Trust was earned screen by screen. No amount of brand messaging would convince someone to deposit money. The product itself had to feel trustworthy.

01 Public marketing site

The brand had to sell itself before users ever logged in.

No physical branches, no ads at launch. The website was the only way in. I led with a savings simulator so visitors could see real numbers before signing up, and replaced industry jargon with plain-language explainers. Every section answers one question: "why should I move my money here?"

02 Savings + deposits

People needed to see their money growing, not just sitting.

I made the earned amount the largest number on the screen. That's the one people actually care about. Push notifications for bonuses and balance changes surface progress without opening the app, turning passive savings into something that feels alive.

03 Investment dashboard

Users couldn't tell if their money was doing well or not.

Competitors showed fund performance as a single percentage. I designed charts that show returns over time, with the data density adapting per breakpoint: desktop shows the full picture, mobile keeps the trend line and key numbers. A glance on any screen should answer "am I up or down?"

04 Risk profile

Regulators required a suitability test. Users hated forms.

Most platforms bury this mandatory test in a 30-field form. I broke it into three progressive sections with one question at a time, a progress indicator, and a "why are we asking this?" link at every step. If users don't understand why you're collecting data, they drop off.

Brand identity

The arrow in the "a".

Brand and product were built at the same time, so every identity decision had to work on a screen. I modified the typeface Nohemi in three places: the "a" became an upward arrow, the "m" flipped into a "w", the "i" lost its dot. That arrow became the app icon, the favicon, and the one element users recognize at any size. I tested it at 32px before it ever went on a billboard.

Impact

What I shipped.

Strategy
Brand approved first round
I presented three directions. The client chose one immediately. No revision cycles, no second-guessing. That early alignment saved weeks and let me move straight into product.
Product
60+ screens across 2 products
A public marketing website and a private investment platform, both responsive across desktop, tablet, and mobile. Shared component system, one design language, all designed and handed off in 4 months.
System
40-page brand manual
Everything the client needed to scale the brand without us: logo, colour, typography, photography, patterns, social templates, and app store assets. Built to outlast the project.
Takeaways

What this project taught me.

When you build brand and product together, both get better.

Every visual decision had to survive a 320px screen. That constraint made the whole system stronger.

In fintech, clarity is the feature.

The UI that explains itself is the UI people finish.

Research doesn't slow you down, it speeds you up.

Two weeks of interviews meant the client approved the brand in one round and I never had to rethink a major product decision.
