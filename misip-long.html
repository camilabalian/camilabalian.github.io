MiSIP

A pregnancy companion built on the PAHO perinatal card, rewritten in the language women actually use for their own bodies.

Client
PAHO
Role
Lead Product Designer
Team

Overview

The brief

PAHO, the Pan American Health Organization, distributes the perinatal card across Latin America. It's the paper document doctors use to track pregnancy, designed by clinicians, for clinicians. PAHO sent it to us as a PDF and asked us to digitize it. One screen. One card. Make it an app.

The product's working name was MiSIP. Mi Sistema de Información Perinatal. Her perinatal record, hers. The name mattered: the pronoun was the entire point.

The women carrying that card weren't clinicians. They were mothers holding a document written in a language that wasn't theirs, trying to understand what was happening inside their own bodies. That gap was the project.

I led design from the first client conversation through MVP delivery, roughly five months end to end, and stayed close as the product moved into continued development with PAHO's team post-launch. On my side I owned the PAHO relationship and the scope argument, the research synthesis, and the card-to-app translation system; two designers on my team owned the design-system production and the post-MVP flow work. One PM, a dev team across iOS and Android, and medical partners from PAHO's perinatal division.

Reframing the brief

I pushed back on the scope

The clinicians commissioning the project had expected the design phase to take two weeks, the time it takes to draw a PDF. Before I could make the scope argument, I had to make the design argument: what designing for a woman who has never used a health app actually involves, and why two weeks gets you a document, not a product.

Once we were speaking the same language, the real question surfaced. A card-only app had no reason to stay on her phone after one use, and I wasn't willing to ship something a woman would open once, feel confused by, and delete. If we were asking her to install it, the product had to earn the space.

I didn't have hard LATAM retention benchmarks to cite, and I wasn't going to invent any. What I had was the shape of the problem: every woman we'd already spoken to named the adjacent apps she'd tried and deleted, and none of them were single-purpose card readers. I drew the argument as two curves and took it into the PAHO meeting. The medical partners pushed back, the card had to stay clinically faithful or they wouldn't validate the data. We landed on the trade that was obvious in hindsight: the card stays as rigorous as the doctors needed, the product owns every layer around it. PAHO agreed.

Research

What women actually asked for

24 expectant mothers and 11 clinicians, across Argentina, Perú, México, and Colombia, in urban, peri-urban, and rural clinics. Semi-structured interviews, in-clinic shadowing, card walkthroughs. Five themes surfaced from synthesis: preocupaciones (worries between appointments), rutinas (the daily routines nobody was tracking), carnet (how the paper card lived in her day), salud (health signals she wanted to read on her own), and otras features (the adjacent needs a card-only app would miss).

Every woman I talked to was navigating pregnancy across fragmented sources: the paper card, WhatsApp groups, Google at 3am, her own mother's advice. She wasn't missing a digital form. She was missing a place where she could understand what was happening to her. The two things she asked for were the two things the brief didn't address: language she could read without a doctor next to her, and one place that held everything instead of seven.

The diagram on the right is a synthesis map recreated for presentation clarity, not a photograph of the original research wall. The initials and ages are anonymized references to the mothers in the cohort.

The decision

We couldn't just translate the card. We had to redesign what it was for.

Copying the card's structure into an app would have reproduced something unreadable. Ignoring the structure entirely would have broken the trust of the medical partners validating the data. Both answers were wrong. We needed a third one.

What was on the table

Digitize faithfully

Preserve the structure and terminology one-to-one. Safe, fast, medically sound. But a pregnant woman opening this app would see the same wall of clinical language, now on her phone instead of paper.

What I argued for

Kept the data.
Rebuilt the language.

Every label rewritten in plain language. Information hierarchy reorganized around what a woman needs to know at each stage of the pregnancy. Progressive disclosure for complexity. Medical partners validated the clinical integrity throughout.

What shipped

A pregnancy companion, in her language.

The digital perinatal card at the center, wrapped in the things women told us they actually needed: appointment tracking, a personal diary, clinical content rewritten in her language, an in-app clinician chat, and a week-by-week progression that lives as its own continuous scroll. Built for iOS and Android on a calm, single-accent system tuned for one-handed use on low-end devices, the reality of most MiSIP users. Adopted by PAHO, currently in use across Latin America.

Moment 01 · Onboarding, her first five minutes

02What this is
03Ready when you are

The first onboarding draft had four consent checkboxes and a terms modal. The shipped version has zero. Three screens that explain who the app is for, in her language, before asking for anything. Consent moved to the moment she adds her own data, not the moment she opens the app. Every "drive completion" draft we tested did worse than this one.

Moment 03 · Gestación actual, the full scroll

Week-by-week was the flow mothers returned to most in research. The question was never "what's happening in week 27", it was "where am I in this." That reframing made the difference between a tab layout and a single continuous scroll: she doesn't navigate to her week, she scrolls until she finds herself. Shown here at the aspect ratio it ships at, end to end, so you can see the whole artifact the way she does.

Impact

What the scope argument bought us

The clearest way to measure this project isn't traffic or DAUs. It's the distance between the brief and the product that shipped, and whether a woman reading her own card comes away understanding it, on her own terms, without a doctor next to her.

In closing

Three things I took from MiSIP.

A medical partner's "no" is usually a question about evidence, not a veto. The hardest part of the first month was sitting with rewrites that kept coming back rejected. I initially read every "no" as a wall. It wasn't. It was the clinicians asking for proof that plain language wouldn't lose the clinical meaning. The moment I started bringing a short comprehension test into review meetings. Two mothers, two versions of a field, their own words. The "nos" turned into "show me the next one." Half the work of senior design on a clinical project is teaching the clinicians how to say yes.

I was three calls late to the scope argument. The two weeks the clinicians had in their heads weren't a misunderstanding about design, they were a misunderstanding I'd let accumulate because I wanted the first meetings to go smoothly. By the time I made the real argument, it cost more to unwind the expectation than it would have cost to set it on day one. Scope fights are cheaper the earlier you pick them, and much cheaper than scope regrets. I pick them on day one now.

The thing I didn't see clearly until month three was that the app had to earn permission, not attention. A pregnancy companion isn't an engagement problem; it's a trust problem. The screens a woman sees in her first five minutes aren't optimizing for activation. They're answering the question "why are you on my phone." Every onboarding draft that tried to "drive completion" tested worse than the one that just said, slowly, who this was for and why. I stopped designing onboarding as a funnel after MiSIP.
